“Confident adulthood” a friend once told me, “is ordering two appetizers for dinner, and no entree.” That is the spirit behind Afternoon Tea: a meal that is not a meal: so we can have whatever we like, a few nibbles at a time, over a couple of hours filled with pleasant company and conversation

The basic structure of Afternoon Tea comprises tea, bread, savouries, sweets and cake; most classically served from a tea-service set on a low table or tea-cart. The actual items served in each category vary broadly, and some suggested seasonal menus follow for such celebrations as Valentines, Family Day, St David's Day, Ladyday, Fenella's Day, Mothering Sunday, a home-coming, Mother's Day, a graduation, a garden party, Father's Day, St-Johnmass, Dominion Day, St Anne's Day, Lammas, Heritage Day, Labour Day, Michaelmas, St Faith's Day, a birthday, St Andrew's Day, Hallowe'en, Stir-up Sunday, or New-year's.

Tea

The mid twenty-teens are a golden age for tea-drinkers in Canada much as the nineteen-seventies were: gourmet tea vendors David's Tea and Teavana are in every mall, and independent tea sellers earn a living in boutique districts. Murchie's mail-order business is, at least for now, no longer the only recourse for those who want to taste an estate Darjeeling or a unique blend. Tisanes and fruit-flavoured tea are one way to introduce variety into your choice of tea, but keep those for your individual mug over breakfast or studying, or as you wind down for bed. For teatime at the Logrus we prefer the more essential variety of different actual teas -- that is, the leaves of the Camillia plant –- grown in different places and prepared by different techniques that result in each the tea having its own unique flavour, perhaps subtly augmented by floral aromas but never over-powered.

Use a large bone-china teapot for afternoon tea, leaving the silver teapot for after-dinner tea when smaller and fewer cups are poured and the pot has less time to cool.

“Hot the Pot” by filling it with hot tap-water, and bring a full kettle to the boil. Afternoon Tea typically lasts three hours and you will pour about three rounds an hour, so ideally have nine dry clean wire-mesh tea-balls or fillable nylon bags ready at hand: although you can make the tea loose in the pot and use a strainer, that makes the second pot much slower to prepare, as you will need to clean all the old leaves out before adding the new leaves to prevent the bitterness that overbrewed leaves give to the tea. A standard Royal Albert teapot is 42 ounces and will pour seven cups of tea. The old rule of “a teaspoon for each person and one for the pot” results in overly-strong tea unless the teaspoon is very scant, and the best quantity of tea per cup varies with the type of tea, so use the amounts given below or on the tea-tin. Empty the warming-water from the pot, put the filled teabag in it, and pour the boiling water over the tea. Allow the tea to steep for the time given below or on the tea-tin. Remove the tea-ball to the slops bowl before pouring to prevent over-steeping.

Top Tier: hot breads

Fill the top tier of the serving rack with hot scones, toast, bonnach, fruit bread and so on. If you are using the three-tier stand that has a central carrying hook and a narrow top plate, cover the hot breads with the tall glass dome; if you are using the stand that has a broad looped handle across the top use the flatter broader glass dome. Since this second stand supports your choice of plates, make sure you use a plate – or bowl, to hold more scones – broad enough to support the glass dome.

Middle Tier: Savouries

Fill the middle tier with small thin sandwiches, sausages (on skewers or wrapped in pastry to keep fingers clean), small meat pastries, miniature souvlaki, hard-boiled eggs. Also fit little bowls of clotted cream, soft butter, and apple jelly onto this tier, with tiny silver spoons to serve the condiments.

Bottom Tier: Sweets

Fill the lowest tier with cake slices and miniature cakes, sliced fruit, tiny tarts, cookies (biscuits). When the scones and savouries are gone, remove this plate from the server and replace it on the table, and set the server aside.

Cake

Choose neat close-crumbed cake with hard or no glaze. Put the cake on the domed glass cake-stand on the lower shelf of the tea-wagon. Move it up to the low table and slice it after the savouries and breads are removed.

Tea: Rwanda Orthodox Black

East African teas epitomize “tea-ness”: their characteristic sweet flavour is usually enhanced by the mechanical cut-tear-curl processing that is near-ubiquitous in East Africa, where “orthodox” refers to tea that has been cured and dried as whole leaves. Orthodox tea will brew more slowly but has a longer shelf-life and a subtly different flavour.

Tea-service

A beautiful sterling silver-service comprising a teapot, kettle on its little alcohol burner, slops bowl, milk jug, sugar basin and tray is a rare luxury. I want one. But my existing four-piece silver-plate service serves me well enough with my little old brass kettle to supplement it and a china bowl pressed into service for the used tea-leaves and bitter pot-dregs. The happy truth is that rare individual unmatched pieces – so-called “harlequin china” is proper and traditional for afternoon tea. In a world where even a classic Royal Doulton “tea service” of pot, creamer and sugar-basin can carry a daunting price, it is a happy thought that the variety of your non-matching cups advertises your traditional values: that you are no nouveau-riche social-climber trying to buy good taste by the perfectly-matched box set. Your creamer and sugar should match; each cup should match its own saucer; the entire service should “match” in that all your cups are good china and not a mix of pottery and hotel-ware. But beyond that you are free to treasure each individual piece and delight in sharing its individual beauty.

Teapot

Brew only one pot of tea at a time, and use it to pour everyone's cup from the same pot. The ceremony of tea is simultaneously democratizing and aristocratic: even as we luxuriate in the first-world wealth that gives us easy access to exotic teas and rich dainties, within the confines of the parlour we are participating in share-and-share alike. And if a prickle of conscience should strike at the thought that our comfortable tea-drinking contrasts sadly with the lives of those who grow and harvest our tea-leaves, let us let that thought inspire us to choose fair-trade teas and invest in those lands in other ways as well.
